Slave scam-centres

A global network of scammers have developed a modern slave trade, taking people and forcing them to work in call centres across south-east Asia. We've been tracking them for years.

By Ivan Franceschini and Ling Li

Published: May 3, 2024 at 5pm AEST

In late 2023, Yun and Pei (pseudonyms), two young people from China’s Sichuan Province, were scrolling Kuaishou, a popular Chinese live-video platform, when they noticed what looked like a promising opportunity.

Barely 17 and 20, they were scraping a living on construction sites around their hometown, and here was a job that promised to pay a daily wage that was nearly double what they were earning, plus food and lodging.

After receiving a first positive response, they embarked on a journey to a coastal city in Guangxi Province for an interview in person. There, their enthusiasm quickly turned to fear, as the supposed prospective employer resorted to threats of violence to coerce them onto a boat.

Within half an hour they were in Vietnam, where they spent a couple of days under guard in a hotel before being transported by car to Bavet, a Cambodian border town.

In Bavet, they were brought to a compound known for hosting both online gambling and cyber-fraud operations and told that they would have to engage in online scams.

Unwilling, the two youths tried to escape but got caught by security guards and beaten badly. After spending several days locked up inside the dormitory, they agreed to learn scam techniques.

Moved to another compound to be trained, in a strike of luck, they managed to contact relatives back in China, who promptly reported to authorities in China and the Chinese Embassy in Cambodia.

Finally, in January of this year, they were rescued by Cambodian police.

A story among many

Over the past few years, we have come across hundreds of stories that are just more or less tragic variations of this one. Individuals of various backgrounds and nationalities find themselves trapped inside compounds in Southeast Asia — mostly in Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, and the Philippines — where they are trained in various scam techniques and then forced to defraud people all over the world.

Some of them are there after being duped or drafted against their will, prevented from leaving, subjected to round-the-clock surveillance, and disciplined through the constant threat of arbitrary physical violence.

Others might have entered willingly but they too find themselves trapped, either through threats of violence or demands for exorbitant fees to break dubious contracts they are required to sign on arrival.

Passports and other documents are taken away upon entrance, which means that they are effectively stranded in a foreign country where they most often don’t know anyone nor speak the language.

The way it has taken shape in the past few years, the online scam industry is unique in that it presents us with two distinct, seldom overlapping sets of victims.

On one side are the targets of the scams, who often lose the savings of a lifetime and go through horrible psychological suffering, an ordeal that leaves them with their sense of self-worth in tatters and, in some cases, on the brink of suicide. **JB: stats on how much has been lost globally due to scams in most recent year**

On the other side, somehow surprisingly, are some of the scammers themselves, people like Yun and Pei, who end up working in scam operations in conditions akin to modern slavery after getting tricked or trafficked.

It is with this latter cohort — which tentative UN estimates put in the hundreds of thousands in Southeast Asia alone — that we have been engaging in the past few years as we attempted to shed light into the inner workings of the online scam industry.

Who are we?

The three of us come from different backgrounds. Ivan Franceschini and Mark Bo have been observing Chinese investment in Southeast Asia, especially Cambodia, since the early 2010s, Ivan researching issues related to labour rights, and Mark with a focus on both political dynamics and social and environmental impacts.

As the online scam industry began to gain prominence in Cambodia during and after the Covid-19 pandemic, and as Chinese-ethnic criminal organisations played a fundamental role in it, it was unavoidable for us to turn our attention to it.

Ling Li instead comes from a background in the humanitarian industry and has been intensively engaging with survivors of the online scam industry since 2022, interacting with local and international civil society organisations to bring them relief and supporting them with repatriation. **JB: can we mention her former role in Chinese law enforcement?**

Now all the interviews and informal conversations that we have had with survivors, members of informal rescue teams, civil society practitioners, law enforcement, along with the wealth of information that we have accumulated in years of observing the power networks and money flows that underpin the industry, have gone into a book, Scam: Inside Southeast Asia’s Cybercrime Industry, which will be published by Verso Books in 2025.

Rise of an Industry

To understand where today’s online scam industry comes from, we need to turn our gaze to Taiwan in the 1990s.

image: 1990s Taiwan

In the second half of the decade, pioneering scammers started experimenting with some of the sophisticated techniques of online fraud that we are still seeing today.

image: scratch card example

In one of the most notorious instances, local crime syndicates created a popular scratch-card lottery in which lottery buyers were led to believe they had won a prize and then asked them to pay taxes on their winnings before collecting.

As law enforcement in Taiwan became increasingly strict, starting from the early 2000s the focus of the scammers turned to mainland China. Online scam cases first made major headlines in China in 2004, with a case involving a well-known retired professor from a university in Beijing. After being scammed out of a significant amount of money for a television set and laptop that he was told were a lottery prize, the man sent a complaint to top leaders in the central government.

This set in motion an investigation that eventually revealed that the scammer was an 18-year-old woman in a county in Fujian Province called Anxi.

Around the same time, another high-profile online fraud case was reported to the police, and the operator behind it also turned out to be based in Anxi.

This was the beginning of the county developing a reputation as a "scam township" (zhapian zhi xiang). Suddenly, the place was receiving huge attention not only from the Chinese media and public, but also from law enforcement bodies.

image: Chinese compound from 2004

According to Chinese media reporting from that time, Anxi’s scam operations worked on a family-based model, supplemented by the hiring of external workers. These employees received either a fixed monthly salary of 500 yuan or a commission ranging from 10 to 20% of the money they generated. They were provided with food and accommodation, usually in a sealed-window suite, a characteristic that eerily recalls the situation of today’s scam compounds, even though we have no evidence that these workers were coerced to perpetrate the scams they engaged in.

More broadly, these early operations already showed one landmark characteristic of online scam operations, that is the fact that, contrary to what many observers of the industry believe, they are mostly run by smaller groups of entrepreneurial Chinese-ethnic criminals, rather than "traditional" organised crime groups, such as Hong Kong triads.

These developments led to increasingly strict law enforcement on the mainland. In the early 2010s, with scammers now having to find new bases for their operations, Southeast Asia — especially Cambodia and the Philippines — emerged as their destinations of choice.

As they reviewed media and police reports of arrests and repatriations of Chinese nationals suspected of online fraud, two Chinese scholars were not able to locate any reports of large-scale arrests in Southeast Asia before 2011, an indication that at that time the industry had not taken roots in the region yet.

They found a spike in arrests in the region only in 2015, in concomitance with a renewed crackdown on online fraud in China.

This gives us a glimpse of the main push factor that leads crime groups to leave one location, that is, the need to escape regulatory changes and police crackdowns.

As for the pull side, scam operators are attracted by regulatory voids, lax law enforcement, and existence of local elites and networks willing to grant their protection. As Chinese-ethnic criminal groups control many of these organisations, proximity to both Taiwan and mainland China is helpful, so that the fraudsters can easily travel back and forth and secure a steady supply of Chinese-speaking workers.

It also helps if the destination has decent existing infrastructure, especially in terms of internet connectivity, telecommunications, and office space. This is the same type of infrastructure that is required for online gambling, which explains why the online scam industry has taken roots in country where online gambling is (or used to be) legally allowed or tolerated as an informal source of revenue.

Zooming In

The gambling-scam nexus is particularly evident in Cambodia and the Philippines. In Cambodia, **where we have done the vast majority of our research,** the government initially allowed online gambling but then suddenly banned it in August 2019, citing public security concerns linked to the industry.

image: old casino?

This move left behind a significant infrastructure ready for scammers to use, especially in places like the coastal city of Sihanoukville, which since 2017 had seen a significant influx of foreign investment in the casino and entertainment industry, mostly coming from Chinese-ethnic actors.

In the Philippines, in 2016 the Duterte administration decided to regulate the industry by establishing the ‘Philippine Offshore Gambling Operations’ (POGO) scheme, a new policy that enabled online gambling firms to legally set up shop in major cities. In the following years, many of these POGOs turned out to be fronts for online scam operations, leading to widespread calls from policymakers to ban the program altogether.

Myanmar presents a different picture. There, law from the 1980s completely prohibited gambling of any sort in the country, and only in 2019 a new law permitted hotels of three stars and above to set up gambling venues for foreigners.

Still, despite the restrictions, some areas did develop casino industries, particularly border areas administered by ethnic armed organisations such as Kokang, Wa State, and Kayin State. However, these regions are not all easily accessible, operation costs are higher than elsewhere in mainland Southeast Asia, and conflict risks high.

In this environment, the online scam industry was likely attracted not so much by the existing infrastructure provided by online gambling, but rather by the opportunity to establish itself in places that are in many regards lawless. These border areas are effectively ruled by warlords often allied with the Myanmar military, who have for years operated myriad criminal operations, many of which are transnational in nature.

The online scam industry experienced intense growth in northern and eastern Myanmar in the wake of the 2021 military coup and in the wake of a short-lived crackdown on the industry in Cambodia in late 2022, but its future is now uncertain due to intense crackdowns started in late 2023 by local actors in collaboration with Chinese law enforcement.

Scaling Up

Since the move to Southeast Asia, the online scam industry has grown exponentially. Although they were already raking in impressive profits, scam operators realised there was still much room for growth — they just had to find ways to secure larger workforces and supporting infrastructure to scale up their activities.

While at the beginning scam operations were often small-scale and mostly located in apartments, villas, and hotel rooms, in the late 2010s they began to coalesce into bigger scam compounds.

These facilities in many cases are purpose-built for online operations, with offices, dormitories, and space for shops, entertainment, and other amenities for workers. In other cases, they are office buildings attached to or in the vicinity of public-facing casinos, or they may occupy the upper floors of a licensed casino. In others again, they are repurposed condominiums and apartments.

By sharing infrastructure and facilities, scam operations have been able to achieve an economy of scale. As they scaled up, scam operations have also become more professional and are now run as ‘proper’ companies, with various departments in charge of different side of the business, such as recruitment, human resources, logistics, and external contractors in charge of more technical aspects, including money laundering.

Many observers cite the Covid-19 pandemic as the factor that triggered the whole scaling up of the online scam industry. While, as we have seen, the transition started years before the virus made its appearance, the pandemic undoubtedly acted as a catalyst.

The widespread baggage of misery caused by the health crisis and the ensuing lockdowns and border closures provided the crime groups that run scam operations with both a larger pool of isolated people vulnerable to their frauds and an immiserated workforce willing to believe any promise and accept any job to supplement their collapsing livelihoods.

Types of Scams

At the root of these scam operations are technological changes that have made increasingly sophisticated frauds feasible.

Southeast Asia’s scam compounds are generally associated with ‘pig butchering’ (shazhupan) frauds, arguably the most well-known—and widely studied—online scam method that has emerged in recent years. **JB: Can we say who the targets of the SE Asia scam compounds are--are they mostly going after people in mainland China and the Chinese diaspora?**

Sitting at the intersection of traditional romance and investment scams, this technique generally entails four stages:

  • pig searching (zhao zhu)
  • pig feeding (wei zhu)
  • pig grooming (yang zhu)
  • pig slaughtering (sha zhu).

First scammers create fake profiles and search for targets (the ‘pigs’) on social media or via ‘wrong number’ messages. Then, once they have found a target, they start grooming them by showing care, sending gifts, and engaging in conversations that in some cases last for months.

Once the target is on the hook, the ‘pig-grooming period’ begins. At that point, the scammers recommend fake investment products or steer them to cryptocurrency or foreign exchange investment platforms that often mimic legitimate apps and sites.

The targets will be shown how to invest, and through the scammer-controlled platforms, they will see their investment steadily increase in value.

‘Pig-slaughtering’ often comes when victims want to cash out or start to get suspicious. At this point, the scammers lock them out of the app and vanish.

A second common strategy has a long history even outside the Chinese-speaking world and, contrarily to what some observers have claimed, and is not linked to the specific political situation of mainland China. This involves the impersonation of law enforcement officials.

For instance, one of our informants, a young Chinese graduate student in the United Kingdom, one day received a call from someone claiming to be from the Chinese Embassy in London who informed her that she was in trouble and the Shanghai Municipal Public Security Bureau wanted her cooperation.

Wary, she checked the number that had popped up on her screen and it matched that of the embassy. The call was transferred to a second person claiming to be from the Shanghai police, who informed her that she was among the suspects in a serious case involving forged passports and money laundering. He warned her not to tell her family or friends because anyone who knew about the situation could be accused of harbouring criminals.

Soon after, another alleged police officer contacted her on the app Telegram, sent her what he claimed was an ‘arrest warrant’, and video called her to collect her testimony.

image/animaton: Telegram message

For almost two months, she had to report her thoughts and activities to this " "officer" every day. After she was completely subjugated and dependent on him, he told her that if she wanted to prove her innocence, she had to transfer RMB500,000 (roughly US$72,500) to a designated "safe account" so that the police could escrow the money until all suspicions about her were cleared. After she did so, the scammers vanished.

Pig butchering and official impersonation scams require not only technological means, but also substantial investments in time and manpower. These frauds are highly scripted, with prospective scammers undergoing intensive training on how to use specific tools and dedicated phrasebooks, which are said to be possibly elaborated by professional psychologist and behavioural specialists.

Other types of scams are more basic. In some cases, scammers lure often inexperienced investors to put their money into buying or exchanging cryptocurrencies on phoney platforms by offering artificially high rates.

In others, they flood dating apps with fake profiles, which are then used to induce targets into participating in compromising intimate chats or video calls.

There are then the classic predatory loans and e-commerce scams that involve offering products for low prices on mainstream online marketplaces to entice victims.

However, scammers can also be remarkably creative. For instance, over the past couple of years, there have been reports of frauds targeting Chinese students with a new bizarre technique. Scammers have been convincing their targets that they or their family are under investigation for financial crimes and persuade them to send them photos and videos of themselves staging their own kidnapping. They then use these materials to extort ransoms from their family members.

image: Kidnapping pic

For instance, in October 2022, two students in their early twenties living in Singapore were contacted by fake police officers who said they were under investigation for spreading misinformation, one about monkeypox and the second about Covid-19 in Guangdong.

They were told they would have to assist in investigations and instructed to travel to Cambodia to conduct ‘missions’. Once in Phnom Penh, they were told to stay in separate hotels, cease all communication with family and friends, and record videos of themselves pretending to be kidnapped for the purpose of scam education and investigations.

The fake kidnapping videos they filmed were sent to the parents with ransom demands totalling RMB2.8 million (US$390,000). Cambodian police located them before any ransoms were paid. **JB: are any of these videos able to be shown?**

Kidnappings are not always staged. In one horrific instance in the summer of 2023, a 23-year-old Chinese student in New Zealand received a call from the ‘Chinese consulate’ saying they had received a complaint that someone was sending harassing texts from a phone number registered with her ID.

She was told to file a police report and transferred to the ‘Chinese police department’.

The fake police asked for her ID number and told her that there was a file on her and that she was also being investigated in a transnational money laundering case. The ‘police officer’ sent various official-looking documents to her, including an arrest warrant. He said that she could avoid investigation if she cooperated as a witness and travelled to Cambodia to identify the people involved.

After arriving in Cambodia, she was taken to a compound near the border with Vietnam, where she was tortured, the video then sent to the family. Only a few days later she was rescued by the Cambodian police, alerted by the family.

With the techniques used by scammers already highly sophisticated, the emergence and accessibility of new artificial intelligence (AI) tools is creating more opportunities.

For at least the past year, media reports have documented scams facilitated by ‘deep fake’ technology, where software is used to mimic the face or voice of someone known to the scam target. Additionally, generative AI tools can make it easier to send out phishing messages using duplicate or created backstories and trails of digital activity to make fake identities seem more believable.

According to the head of the financial and economic crime unit at Europol, with large-language tools like ChatGPT ‘you can write thousands of tailored messages in different languages with different targets, different stories, and you can do that with your laptop from wherever.’

Going Global

If the past few years spent observing the online scam industry have taught us anything, it is that the capital behind these operations is very mobile.

As we have seen, scam operators seek locations where there is existing infrastructure they can take advantage of, such as reliable internet connectivity and telecommunication links, as well as office and accommodation space, ideally in coastal or border areas.

If the physical infrastructure is lacking, it can still be created, but what is most essential is for operators to build connections with local elites and power brokers or tap into networks that have them. These connections are essential to ensure the ability of the scam compounds to continue to operate and generally exist in contexts where law enforcement is lax and corruption is rampant.

This type of capital is also particularly sensitive to regulatory crackdowns, moving from one country to the next in a perpetual search for places where it can operate with impunity.

When scam operators come under pressure in some areas—as happened, briefly, in Cambodia in September 2022 and then again in late 2023 in northern Myanmar—they are often able to simply pack up and move to a different location. Dozens of countries can provide a suitable base to these groups, as long as they can count on basic internet infrastructure and the protection and support of local friendly elites and complicit state actors.

chart/map: new scam locations

Today fraud operations have gone truly global, with evidence of scam compounds appearing as far afield as Serbia, Turkey, Georgia, Mexico, Peru, and West Africa. As new technologies are developed and advance, online scams are only likely to become more sophisticated.

While Chinese-ethnic criminal groups remain a dominant force in the sector, they are now flanked by a very diverse and growing cohort of local and transnational criminals, businessmen, corrupt state officials, politicians, and law enforcement officials from all over the world.

Likewise, there will never be any lack of people who are willing to travel to remote places following the mirage of making easy money—whether they know what they are getting into or not. Compound capitalism might change form again, but it is here to stay.
