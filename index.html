'We could hear the screams'

Inside the slavery scam centres of Southeast Asia

By Ivan Franceschini and Ling Li

Published: May 3, 2024 at 5pm AEST

Late last year, Yun and Pei (pseudonyms), two young people from China’s Sichuan Province, were scrolling Kuaishou, a popular Chinese video platform, when they noticed a promising opportunity in the construction sector.

Just 17 and 20 years old, they were scraping out a living on building sites around their hometown. The job advertisement promised to pay nearly double their current daily wage, plus food and lodging for similar work.

After receiving a positive response through the app, they embarked on a journey to a coastal city in Guangxi Province hundreds of kilometres away for an interview in person.

Their enthusiasm quickly turned to fear, however, as the supposed prospective employer threatened them with violence if they did not board a boat.

Within half an hour they were in Vietnam, where they spent a couple of days under guard in a hotel before being transported by car to Bavet, a Cambodian border town.

Here, they discovered the job they had been recruited to do. Yun and Pei were taken to a compound hosting both online gambling and cyber-fraud operations. They were then told they would have to engage in online scams.

Unwilling, they tried to escape but got caught by security guards and were beaten badly. After being locked up in a dormitory for several days, they finally agreed to learn scam techniques.

In a stroke of luck, they were soon moved to another compound and managed to contact relatives back in China through WeChat. Their families promptly reported their situation to the authorities in China and the Chinese embassy in Cambodia.

Finally, in January of this year, they were rescued by Cambodian police.

A story among many

In our extensive research into the online scam industry in Southeast Asia over the past few years, we have come across countless stories similar to this one.

After travelling to Southeast Asia – sometimes willingly, other times through deception or trafficking networks – thousands of people from all walks of life have found themselves trapped inside compounds, subjected to around-the-clock surveillance and subjected to constant threats of arbitrary physical violence.

These scam centre victims mostly come from the Chinese-speaking world, especially mainland China and Taiwan, but nongovernmental organisations have documented stories involving victims from dozens of other countries.

Their passports and other documents are often confiscated, meaning they are effectively stranded in a foreign country where they might not know anyone or speak the language. They are also required to sign dubious contracts, which they cannot break without paying exorbitant fees.

Working in what can best be described as slave-like conditions, with little to no pay and no access to the outside world, they are then forced to defraud people all over the world through various scam techniques.

Some endure sexual violence. A Taiwanese woman named Alice (pseudonym) told us that when she refused to engage in scam work, she was sold numerous times between compounds and repeatedly raped. Before she was finally rescued, the gangsters even threatened to lock her up in a brothel-like clubhouse at a scam compound to serve at the pleasure of the managers and workforce.

The burgeoning online scam industry is unique in that it creates two distinct, seldom overlapping sets of victims.

On one side are the targets of the scams, who can lose their lifetime savings, leaving their sense of self-worth in tatters and, in some cases, pushing them to suicide.

The size of the industry is huge. In the United States alone in 2022, losses from investment fraudscams reported by the public to the FBI’s Internet Crimes Complaint Center totalled US$3.31 billion (nearly A$5 billion). Scams involving cryptocurrency, including one commonly called "pig butchering" which we'll describe later, were the most common, resulting in US$2.57 billion (A$3.85 billion) in reported losses (a 183% increase from the previous year).

In 2023, the authorities in China managed to stop scams valuing some 328.8 billion yuan, roughly US$45.29 billion (A$68 billion). **is there any estimate on how many they have not managed to stop?**

On the other side are the scammers themselves, people like Yun, Pei and Alice. The United Nations estimates the total number of victims in these compounds may number in the hundreds of thousands. These are the stories we've endeavoured to tell when we started researching the scam industry several years ago.

How we conducted our research

The three lead researchers on our project come from very different backgrounds.

image: researchers?

Ivan Franceschini has been researching Chinese investment in Southeast Asia, especially Cambodia, since the early 2010s, with a focus on labour rights.

Ling Li comes from a background in Chinese law enforcement and the humanitarian sector. For the past two years, she has worked with civil society organisations in Cambodia to help survivors of the online scam industry and support them with repatriation to their home countries.

And Mark Bo, an independent researcher, has worked with local and international non-governmental organisations, development agencies and UN agencies in Cambodia, with a focus on corporate and financial accountability.

As the online scam industry began to gain prominence in Cambodia during and after the outbreak of the COVID pandemic, our research focus started to shift. We are now publishing a book in early 2025 documenting our research, entitled Scam: Inside Southeast Asia’s Cybercrime Compounds.

The book relies heavily on in-depth interviews that Ling conducted between 2022 and 2024 with 71 survivors of compounds mostly located in Cambodia, Laos and Myanmar. Through their words, we've been able to learn about the inner workings of the compounds, how the industry is structured and what the victims have endured.

We've also scoured instant messaging apps, online job advertisement websites and social media platforms to understand how criminal groups recruit people into the operations. Through extensive interviews and engagement with rescue teams, civil society practitioners and law enforcement officers, we’ve also gained insight into how difficult it is to get people out of the compounds and shut down them down.

Finally, we've also accumulated a wealth of data through years of observing the power networks and money flows that underpin the industry. It was surprising to us how much information was out there, if you knew where to look.

Rise of an Industry

To understand how the global online scam industry got started, we need to go back to the early days of the internet in Taiwan in the 1990s. As the internet began to develop here, so did the scam industry – scammers began experimenting with increasingly sophisticated techniques to defraud unwitting targets.

As law enforcement in Taiwan began cracking down on the industry in the early 2000s, many scammers shifted their attention to mainland China. And one county in southern Fujian Province called Anxi, in particular, soon became synonymous with online fraud. It eventually became known in Chinese media reports as a "scam township" (zhapian zhi xiang).

According to reporting from the time, Anxi’s scam operations often worked on a family-based model, supplemented by the hiring of external workers. These employees received either a fixed monthly salary or commission based on the money they brought in, along with food and accommodation. This basic set-up resembles the way today's massive scam compounds in Southeast Asia are organised, though we have no evidence these early workers were coerced or held against their will.

When police in China started to close in on these scam operations in the early 2010s, they uprooted again – this time for Southeast Asia, in particular Cambodia and the Philippines.

What attracted the operators to these countries – and later, Myanmar, Laos and others – is the lack of regulations, lax law enforcement and the existence of protective umbrellas of local elites willing to shield them from scrutiny.

According to Chinese researchers, the operations were also largely still run by groups from Taiwan at this time, so the proximity to both Taiwan and mainland China facilitated a steady supply of Chinese-speaking workers. Later on, criminal groups from the mainland would become the most prominent forces in the industry.

In addition, these scam operations require decent infrastructure, especially in terms of internet connectivity, telecommunications and office space – the same conditions that have allowed the online gambling industry to flourish.

This also explains why Cambodia, in particular, has become a hotbed for scammers – online gambling was initially legal or at least tolerated by the government here.

Explosive growth

This changed, though, in August 2019. Cambodian authorities suddenly banned online gambling, citing public security concerns linked to the industry. While many operations continued, others abruptly shut down and moved out.

This left behind a significant infrastructure ready for scammers to use solely for their own operations. This was especially true in places like the coastal city of Sihanoukville, which had seen a significant influx of foreign investment in the casino and entertainment industry, mostly coming from ethnic Chinese sources.

In the Philippines, former President Rodrigo Duterte enacted a new policy in 2016 to allow for legal online gambling operations in major cities, in an effort to regulate the industry. However, in the ensuing years, many of these became fronts for far more lucrative scam operations.

In Myanmar, it's a different picture. Gambling has been outlawed there since the 1980s, with only a slight easing in 2019 when upscale hotels were permitted to establish casinos for foreigners.

Despite the restrictions, some areas did develop gambling industries, particularly in the border areas controlled by ethnic armed organisations, such as Kokang, Wa state and Kayin state. These areas are effectively ruled by warlords often allied with the Myanmar military, who have for years operated myriad criminal operations. In such an environment, online scam operations were also able to thrive.

The growth of the KK Park compound on the Myanmar-Thailand border is illustrative of how quickly the industry has grown here.

As recently as October 2019, satellite images show the area where KK Park now sits to be undisturbed agricultural land.

Construction began soon after.

Here it is in November 2021

And one year later.

In just a few years, the area became one of Myanmar’s most notorious scam compounds.

As of early 2024, the area known as KK Park and newer adjacent compounds covered almost more than 7.5 square kilometres of land.

The already rapidly expanding industry then boomed in northern and southeastern Myanmar in the wake of the 2021 military coup. Late last year, ethnic militias captured military-held towns in northern Shan state, leading to an intense crackdown on scam operations in collaboration with Chinese law enforcement. But despite the repatriation of almost 50,000 Chinese nationals, the industry continues to operate.**source here**

Cyber-scam operations have also found a home in Laos. The vast majority of these compounds are located in the Golden Triangle Special Economic Zone, which has long been a notorious hub for various forms of transnational crime, such as illegal wildlife and drug trafficking.

Although it is harder to pinpoint their exact locations, scam operations also exist elsewhere in the country. We have found Chinese reports of arrests and deportations of Chinese nationals who have worked at compounds in the capital, Vientiane, and other locations.

Locations of scam compounds we've found

The sheer size of the industry makes it extremely difficult to assess exactly how many compounds exist in total, but through our monitoring of media reports, court and police reports, and Telegram channels, in addition to our victim testimonies, we have been able to identify more than 270 sites in Cambodia, Myanmar and Laos alone. There will no doubt be many more that have not crossed our radar.

'We could hear the screams continuing until midnight'

As the scam industry began to grow across Southeast Asia, the criminal syndicates running the compounds realised they could take advantage of the latest technological advancements and favourable local conditions to scale up their activities to make even more money.

At the beginning, scam operations were often small-scale and mostly located in apartments, villas and hotel rooms. By the mid-2010s, however, they began to coalesce into bigger, purpose-built compounds, with offices, dormitories, shops, entertainment venues and other amenities for workers. Some were established in buildings attached to or near licensed casinos.

As they've scaled up, some scam operations have also become more professional and are now run as "proper" companies. They now have departments in charge of different sides of the business, such as recruitment, human resources, and logistics, with external contractors in charge of more technical aspects, such as money laundering.

However, unlike companies in legitimate sectors, the compounds exert strict control over their workforce through round-the-clock surveillance and a "carrot and stick" approach to discipline.

On the carrot side, scam centre operators offer bonuses and other rewards to workers, such as access to drugs and sex in clubs and brothels inside the compounds.

In the words of one of our informants, a Chinese man named Tao (pseudonym) who was trapped in a scam compound in Cambodia:

The ‘excellent employee’ would get monetary awards, promotions and sometimes the privilege to temporarily exit the compound. If you were one of them, they would buy you good food and take you to gamble or even pay for prostitutes to let you taste their rich life.

On the stick side, we found overwhelming evidence of torture and violence in the scam compounds.

For those who could not meet their targets, however, life would quickly become a nightmare. These workers were beaten, subjected to electric shocks and in some cases even had their nails extracted. **this came from your interviews? or another source?**

As Tao recounted to us:

We could hear the screams continuing until midnight […] They made sure you understand that you do not want to be the next one to end up in the punishment room.

Types of Scams

The scams that are being perpetrated inside Southeast Asia’s scam compounds originally targeted people in the Chinese-speaking world, especially mainland China. However, in recent years, they have gone truly global.

At the root of this shift are technological changes that have made increasingly sophisticated frauds feasible online, complemented by the availability of an increasingly internationalised workforce.

One of the most common scams is known as "pig butchering" (or sha zhu pan in Mandarin) within the industry.

This technique sits at the intersection of traditional romance and investment scams. It generally entails four stages:

1: pig searching (zhao zhu)
2: pig feeding (wei zhu)
3: pig grooming (yang zhu)
4: pig slaughtering (sha zhu).

First, scammers create fake profiles and search for targets (the "pigs") on social media or via wrong number text messages.

Once they have found a target, they start grooming their victims by engaging in long conversations, some of which can last for months, and sometimes even sending gifts.

Then, the "pig-grooming period" begins, when the scammers try to persuade their marks to take part in fake investment, cryptocurrency or foreign exchange schemes on platforms they have created and manage.

To keep their victims on the hook, these apps or websites will appear to show their investments gaining value.

When a victim begins to get suspicious or asks to cash out, the scammers will move to "pig slaughtering" – locking them out of the app and vanishing.

A second common strategy involves the impersonation of law enforcement officials.

For instance, one of our informants, a young Chinese graduate student in the United Kingdom, told us about a call she received from someone claiming to be from the Chinese embassy in London. The caller said she was in trouble.

The student was immediately suspicious and checked the number on the embassy website – it matched. Scammers do this by using caller ID “spoofing” technology. The call was then transferred to someone claiming to be from the Shanghai police, who told the student she was a suspect in a case involving forged passports and money laundering.

Later, another "police officer" sent her what he claimed was an arrest warrant on Telegram and set up a video call to record her "testimony".

The calls continued daily for almost two months. Eventually, the student fell under the "officer's" complete control. When he told her to transfer RMB500,000 (roughly US$72,500, or A$108,000) to a designated "safe account", which would be held in escrow until the allegations against her were cleared up, she did. The scammers then disappeared.

These scams require substantial investments in time and manpower. They are highly scripted frauds that entail intensive training on how to use specific tools and dedicated phrasebooks, such as the one below.

Other scams are more basic and involve smaller amount of money but at large volume. These include e-commerce scams, parcel delivery phishing scams and predatory loan scams.

Scammers can also be remarkably creative. For instance, Hong Kong police issued an alert in February 2023 targeting animal lovers. One executive had been duped out of cryptocurrency worth HK$6 million (roughly US$770,000, or A$1.15 million) after responding to a post about an abandoned kitten up for adoption in Thailand. She paid delivery and other costs upfront, but was then told the kitten died on the way. She lost more money when she was asked to pay an administrative fee to claim insurance compensation.

In recent years, we also found reports of frauds targeting Chinese students with a bizarre new technique – staging their own kidnappings.

image/video?: kidnapping

For instance, in October 2022, two students living in Singapore were contacted by fake police officers who said they were being investigated for spreading misinformation online about monkeypox and COVID.

They were told to fly to Cambodia to assist in the investigation, but once there, were forced to make videos pretending to be kidnapped, ostensibly as part of a “scam education” exercise. Instead, the videos were sent to their parents with ransom demands totalling RMB2.8 million (US$390,000, or A$585,000). They were rescued by Cambodian police before any ransom was paid.

Kidnappings are not always staged. According to Chinese-language media in Cambodia, in 2023 a 23-year-old Chinese **woman in Shanghai** was similarly contacted by fake police and told she was under investigation for money laundering and sending harassing text messages.

The officer sent what appeared to be official-looking documents, including an arrest warrant, but was told if she agreed to be a witness in the investigation, she could avoid trouble. She flew to Cambodia and was taken to a compound near the Vietnamese border where she was tortured. **This included her finger being chopped off with a knife.** A video was sent to her family with a demand for a ransom of 5 million yuan (US$688,000, or A$1 million). She was later rescued by local police.

Going Global

If our research over the past few years has taught us anything, it's that the capital behind these operations is very mobile.

While physical infrastructure is key to these operations, so is the access to local political and business elites and powerbrokers. In countries like Cambodia, where law enforcement is lax and corruption endemic, these connections ensure that scam compounds can continue to operate.

And when the authorities do launch a crackdown – which happened briefly in Cambodia in late 2022 and in northern Myanmar in late 2023 – the mobile nature of the industry allows scammers to pack up and move to a new location quickly. This relocation may be within the country or across borders. Scam operations have expanded to places like Serbia, Turkey, Georgia, Mexico, Peru and west Africa in recent years, among others.

As new technologies continue to be developed – such as artificial intelligence – online scams are only likely to become more sophisticated and widespread.

While ethnic Chinese criminal groups remain a dominant force in the sector, they are now flanked by a very diverse and growing cohort of local and transnational criminals, businessmen, corrupt state officials, politically connected elites and law enforcement officials all over the world.

Likewise, there will never be any lack of people who are willing to travel to remote places with the lure of making easy money, whether they know what they are getting into or not. In part two of our series, we'll look more deeply at the victims who work in these compounds (either voluntarily or under duress), how they are recruited, what they experienced inside, and how they managed to escape.

These scam compounds will continue to evolve, but the industry on the whole is no doubt here to stay.
