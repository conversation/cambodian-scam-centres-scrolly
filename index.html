‘We own you.
We bought you':

Inside Southeast Asia's
slavery scam centres

By Ivan Franceschini and Ling Li
Photos by Roun Ry

Published: May 3, 2024 at 5pm AEST

George, a young Ugandan man, led a relatively normal life in Kampala. An IT specialist, he fixed computers, configured routers and the like.

Then COVID hit and that normality, along with his work, disappeared.

Like many Africans, George hoped to change his fortunes in Dubai.

But life there was difficult and after two years, he was still struggling to make ends meet.

Then in August 2022, a friend sent him a well-paid IT job opportunity in Laos and he jumped at it. The job was a six-month contract offering US$1,500 per month, plus commissions and free accommodation. Everything seemed to check out.

George was soon on his way to the Golden Triangle, a lawless area on Laos’ border with Myanmar and Thailand.

Once there, he was asked to sign a contract completely different from what he had discussed in Dubai, which required him to pay a substantial amount of money.

His passport and phone were taken away and he was introduced to his new job: scamming people into putting money into fake investments.

For two months, Frank dragged his feet, insisting that he was simply an IT guy and he should do the work he was good at. Then, one day he was sold to a second scam company in Myanmar.

They don't tell you [how much they sell you for]. They just tell you: ‘We own you. We bought you.’ …

It’s weird. It takes you back to the, the ages of the slave trade in Africa. It was hard to believe when someone tells you: ‘I own you.’

He was supposed to be gone only for six months — it would take him an entire year to find his way back to Kampala.

An industry with victims on both sides

We have come across countless stories like George’s in our extensive research into the online scam industry in Southeast Asia over the past few years.

After travelling to Southeast Asia – sometimes willingly, other times through deception or trafficking networks – thousands of people from all walks of life have found themselves trapped inside compounds, subjected to around-the-clock surveillance and constant threats of arbitrary physical violence.

After arriving at compounds in countries like Cambodia and Myanmar, their passports are often confiscated, meaning they are effectively stranded in a place where they don’t know anyone or speak the language. They are required to sign dubious contracts, which they cannot break without paying exorbitant fees.

Some even endure sexual violence. A Taiwanese woman named Alice (a pseudonym) told us that when she refused to engage in scam work, she was sold numerous times between compounds and repeatedly raped. Before she was finally rescued, the gangsters threatened to lock her up in a brothel-like clubhouse at a scam compound to serve at the pleasure of the managers and workforce.

The booming online scam industry is unusual in that it creates two distinct sets of victims.

On one side are the targets of the scams, who can lose their lifetime savings, leaving their sense of self-worth in tatters and, in some cases, pushing them to suicide.

On the other side are the scammers themselves, people like George and Alice. The United Nations estimates the total number of people working in slavery-like conditions in these compounds may number in the hundreds of thousands.

These are the stories we’ve endeavoured to tell.

How we conducted our research

The two of us, alongside our third research partner, Mark Bo, have been tracking the scam industry in Cambodia for years. Our research will be published in a book in early 2025, Scam: Inside Southeast Asia’s Cybercrime Compounds.

The book relies heavily on in-depth interviews that Ling conducted between 2022 and 2024 with over 70 survivors of compounds mostly located in Cambodia, Laos and Myanmar. Through their words, we've been able to document the inner workings of the industry and what the victims endure inside the compounds.

We’ve also scoured instant messaging apps, online job advertisement websites and social media platforms to understand how criminal groups recruit people into the industry. Through extensive interviews and engagement with rescue teams, civil society practitioners and law enforcement officers, we’ve also gained insight into how difficult it is to get people out of the compounds and shut them down.

Because the compounds are strictly off-limits to the public, we hired a photographer in 2022 to shoot aerial images from drones.

Luckily, this trip to Sihanoukville, a Cambodian coastal city, coincided with a short-lived government crackdown on the industry.

The relative quiet allowed us access to places where scammers had once operated.

The images revealed unassuming blocks,
hidden in plain sight...

and at the same time
the enormous scale of the industry.

Finally, we’ve also accumulated a wealth of data through years of observing the power networks and money flows that underpin the industry. It was surprising to us how much information was out there, if you knew where to look.

Rise of an Industry

The global online scam industry got its start in the 1990s in Taiwan, but soon shifted to mainland China and in recent years, South-East Asia.

What attracted operators to countries like Cambodia, the Philippines, Myanmar and Laos was the lack of regulations, lax law enforcement and a protective umbrella of local elites willing to shield them from scrutiny. The close proximity to Taiwan and China also ensured a steady supply of Chinese-speaking workers.

In addition, these scam operations require decent infrastructure, especially internet connectivity, telecommunications and office space. As a result, many emerged in connection with casinos or online gambling operations, which were initially legal or at least tolerated in places like Cambodia and the Philippines.

In Cambodia, Sihanoukville became one of the epicentres of the scamming world, enabled in part by a massive influx of Chinese investment in the gambling and entertainment industry. Overnight, the once-sleepy city became awash with neon, with casinos, high-end hotels and condos going up everywhere.

When the Cambodian government suddenly cracked down on online gambling in 2019, however, the local economy took a significant hit. Hundreds of construction projects were left unfinished, littering the city with half-built skeletons. Many gambling operators were abruptly shut down, leaving behind their infrastructure for scammers to use for their operations.

It's a different story in Myanmar. Gambling was long outlawed here, but some casinos did develop in the areas on the Chinese and Thai borders controlled by ethnic armed groups. These areas have become a hub for myriad criminal operations, including the online scamming industry.

The industry boomed in the border areas when civil war broke out following the 2021 military coup. The Chinese government has grown increasingly frustrated as the scam centres have flourished, entrapping tens of thousands of Chinese workers and shielding wanted fugitives.

The growth of the KK Park scam compound on the Myanmar-Thailand border is illustrative of how quickly the industry has grown.

As recently as October 2019, satellite images show the area to be agricultural land.

Construction began soon after.

In just a few years, the area became one of Myanmar’s most notorious scam compounds.

As of early 2024, KK Park and other adjacent compounds covered some 7.5 square kilometres of land.

The sheer size of the industry makes it difficult to assess exactly how many compounds exist in the region in total. Through our monitoring of media reports, court and police reports, and Telegram channels, in addition to our victim testimonies, we have identified more than 270 sites in Cambodia, Myanmar and Laos alone. There will no doubt be many more that have not crossed our radar.

Shadowy figures pulling the strings

As the scam industry has taken root in Southeast Asia, the criminal syndicates realised they could quickly scale up their activities.

Scam operations were initially small-scale in nature, mostly located in apartments, villas and hotel rooms. By the mid-2010s, however, they began to coalesce in bigger, purpose-built compounds, with offices, dormitories, shops, entertainment venues and other amenities for workers.

The larger scam operations have become more professional and are now run like “proper” companies. They have departments in charge of different sides of the business, such as design, maintenance and troubleshooting the software that they use, and external platforms facilitate money laundering.

In many cases, it is extremely challenging to identify who is at the top of the hierarchy. While it is clear that well-established criminal figures and triad groups are involved, the industry appears to be dominated by a broad cohort of business-oriented groups that enter into partnerships with one another and the local elites where they operate.

Many of these groups are led by ethnic Chinese individuals, in some cases fugitives wanted by the Chinese government who have acquired multiple citizenships. But they are flanked by a very diverse cohort.

For instance, in Cambodia, researchers and journalists have compiled substantial evidence tying scam compounds to local politicians and businesspeople, which allows them to operate without running afoul of local law enforcement. In addition, these powerful elites are increasingly becoming financiers and owners of scam compounds themselves, rather than just partners for Chinese groups.

Far from shying away from the spotlight, some of these actors lead very public lives. They extensively document their flamboyant lifestyles on social media and openly brag about making donations to charity initiatives run by entities linked to local authorities, if not to local law enforcement itself.

In Cambodia, the police largely turn a blind eye to the industry. In some cases, police officials have suggested the compounds simply cannot be policed. One officer told a Cambodian news outlet in 2022: "These places don’t allow us to go in".

There is also ample evidence from our victim testimonies that compound managers have a direct line of communication with the police. We were in Sihanoukville when the 2022 government crackdown on the industry occurred, for instance, and we saw how quickly the compounds were able to shut down and transfer their staff before any raids occurred. They clearly had been given advance notice.

Types of scams

The scams themselves originally targeted people in the Chinese-speaking world, especially mainland China. However, in recent years, they have gone global.

In China, one of the biggest target nations, authorities managed to stop scams valuing some 328.8 billion yuan in 2023, roughly US$45.29 billion (A$68 billion).

In the United States, losses from investment fraud scams in 2022 reported to the FBI’s Internet Crimes Complaint Center totalled US$3.31 billion (nearly A$5 billion).

Scams involving cryptocurrency, including those commonly called “pig butchering”, were the most common. These resulted in reported losses of US$2.57 billion (A$3.85 billion) (a 183% increase from the previous year).

“Pig butchering” (or sha zhu pan in Mandarin) is one of the scam techniques that has attracted the most attention. This technique sits at the intersection of traditional romance and investment scams. It generally entails four stages:

  1. pig finding (zhao zhu)
  2. pig feeding (wei zhu)
  3. pig grooming (yang zhu)
  4. pig slaughtering (sha zhu)

First, scammers create fake profiles and search for targets (the "pigs") on social media or by sending "wrong number" text messages. Once they find a target, they start "feeding" their victims attention. They engage in long conversations, some of which last for months. They also send the victims gifts.

Then, the "pig-grooming period" begins, when the scammers try to persuade their marks to take part in fake investment, cryptocurrency or foreign exchange schemes on platforms they have created. To keep their victims on the hook, these apps or websites will appear to show their investments initially gaining value.

When a victim begins to get suspicious or asks to cash out, the scammers will move to "pig slaughtering" – locking them out of the app and vanishing.

One pig butchering scam victim who spoke to us described how she was approached through WhatsApp.

One day after this innocuous exchange the scammer got back in touch...

Then the scammer began messaging the victim daily, sharing details of his life. Over the course of 2-3 months, they became quite close. The scammer presented himself as a succesful investor in cryptocurrency, futures contracts, and similar ventures – he even shared a photo.

As their relationship deepened, the scammer encouraged the victim to invest with him.

After the victim invested her money, she soon realised that she couldn't withdraw her so-called 'profits'. She reported this issue to the scammer.

Predictably, the scammer then cut all ties and deleted her from his contacts.

Increasingly dangerous scams

Another common strategy involves the impersonation of law enforcement officials.

For instance, one of our informants, a young Chinese graduate student in the United Kingdom, told us about a call she received from someone claiming to be from the Chinese embassy in London.

The student was told she was in trouble. She was immediately suspicious and checked the number on the embassy website – it matched. Scammers do this by using caller ID “spoofing” technology. The call was then transferred to someone claiming to be from the Shanghai police, who said the student was a suspect in a case involving forged passports and money laundering.

Later, another “police officer” sent her what he claimed was an arrest warrant on Telegram and set up a video call to record her “testimony”.

The calls continued daily for almost two months. Eventually, the student fell under the scammer’s complete control. He told her to transfer RMB500,000 (roughly A$108,000) to a designated “safe account”, which would be held in escrow until the allegations against her were cleared up. She complied – and the scammers vanished.

In recent years, the media has been awash with disturbing reports of a new scam technique – persuading young Chinese victims to stage their own kidnappings.

For instance, in October 2022, two students living in Singapore were contacted by fake police officers who said they were being investigated for spreading misinformation about mpox and COVID.

They were told to fly to Cambodia to assist in the investigation, but once there, were forced to make videos pretending to be kidnapped. The videos were then sent to their parents with ransom demands totalling RMB2.8 million (A$585,000). They were rescued by Cambodian police before any ransom was paid.

Kidnappings are not always staged. According to Chinese-language media in Cambodia, a 23-year-old Chinese student in New Zealand was similarly contacted by fake police and told she was under investigation for money laundering and sending harassing text messages.

She received what appeared to be official-looking documents, including an arrest warrant, but was told if she agreed to be a witness, she would avoid prosecution. After she flew to Cambodia, she was whisked to a compound where she was tortured. This included her finger being chopped off with a cleaver.

A video was sent to her family with a demand for a ransom of 5 million yuan (A$1 million). She was later rescued by local police.

Going global

If our research over the past few years has taught us anything, it's that the capital behind these operations is very mobile.

When the authorities do launch a crackdown – which happened briefly in Cambodia in late 2022 – scammers can pack up and move to a new location quickly. Scam operations frequently move within the region, but have also expanded to Serbia, Turkey, Georgia, Mexico, Peru and west Africa in recent years.

as new technologies continue to be developed – such as artificial intelligence – online scams are only likely to become more sophisticated and widespread.

Likewise, there will never be a lack of people willing to travel to remote places with the lure of making easy money, whether they know what they are getting into or not. In part two of our series, we'll look more deeply at the victims who work in these compounds (either voluntarily or under duress), how they are recruited and what happens to them inside.

These scam compounds will continue to evolve, but the industry on the whole is no doubt here to stay.
